<p style="color: #1565C0; font-weight: 700; font-size: 1.08rem; margin-top: 0; margin-bottom: 14px;">Question 1</p>

<p style="color: #333; line-height: 1.75;">
A charge of 45 millicoulombs passes through a cross-section of wire in 9 seconds. What is the current?
</p>

<div class="upper-alpha" markdown>

1. 5 A
2. 5 mA
3. 0.2 A
4. 405 mA

</div>

<details style="margin-top: 1rem;">
<summary style="color: #5A3EED; font-weight: 700; cursor: pointer;">Show Answer</summary>
<div style="background: #E7F7E7; border: 2px solid #81C784; border-radius: 10px; padding: 18px 22px; margin-top: 10px;">
<p style="color: #2E7D32; font-weight: 700; margin-top: 0;">Correct Answer: B</p>
<p style="color: #333; line-height: 1.75; margin-bottom: 0;">
Using \(I = Q/t\): \(I = 45 \times 10^{-3} \text{ C} \div 9 \text{ s} = 5 \times 10^{-3} \text{ A} = 5 \text{ mA}\). Don't forget to keep units consistent — the charge is in millicoulombs, so the current is in milliamps.
</p>
<p style="color: #555; font-style: italic; margin-bottom: 0; margin-top: 8px;">
<strong>Concept Tested:</strong> Electric Current Definition \(I = Q/t\)
</p>
</div>
</details>

<p style="color: #1565C0; font-weight: 700; font-size: 1.08rem; margin-top: 0; margin-bottom: 14px;">Question 2</p>

<p style="color: #333; line-height: 1.75;">
Voltage is formally defined as which of the following?
</p>

<div class="upper-alpha" markdown>

1. The rate of charge flow past a point
2. Energy per unit time delivered to a circuit element
3. Work done per unit charge moved between two points
4. The opposition to current flow in a material

</div>

<details style="margin-top: 1rem;">
<summary style="color: #5A3EED; font-weight: 700; cursor: pointer;">Show Answer</summary>
<div style="background: #E7F7E7; border: 2px solid #81C784; border-radius: 10px; padding: 18px 22px; margin-top: 10px;">
<p style="color: #2E7D32; font-weight: 700; margin-top: 0;">Correct Answer: C</p>
<p style="color: #333; line-height: 1.75; margin-bottom: 0;">
Voltage is defined as \(V = W/Q\), the energy (work) in joules required to move one coulomb of charge between two points. Choice A defines current, choice B defines power, and choice D defines resistance. Voltage is always a potential difference between two points, never an absolute value at a single point.
</p>
<p style="color: #555; font-style: italic; margin-bottom: 0; margin-top: 8px;">
<strong>Concept Tested:</strong> Voltage Definition \(V = W/Q\)
</p>
</div>
</details>

</div>

<p style="color: #1565C0; font-weight: 700; font-size: 1.08rem; margin-top: 0; margin-bottom: 14px;">Question 3</p>

<p style="color: #333; line-height: 1.75;">
A 12 V source is connected to a 4 kΩ resistor. Using Ohm's Law, how much power is dissipated in the resistor?
</p>

<div class="upper-alpha" markdown>

1. 48 mW
2. 36 mW
3. 3 mW
4. 48 W

</div>

<details style="margin-top: 1rem;">
<summary style="color: #5A3EED; font-weight: 700; cursor: pointer;">Show Answer</summary>
<div style="background: #E7F7E7; border: 2px solid #81C784; border-radius: 10px; padding: 18px 22px; margin-top: 10px;">
<p style="color: #2E7D32; font-weight: 700; margin-top: 0;">Correct Answer: B</p>
<p style="color: #333; line-height: 1.75; margin-bottom: 0;">
Using \(P = V^2/R\): \(P = (12)^2 / 4000 = 144/4000 = 0.036 \text{ W} = 36 \text{ mW}\). You can verify with current first: \(I = 12/4000 = 3 \text{ mA}\), then \(P = VI = 12 \times 0.003 = 36 \text{ mW}\). The \(P = V^2/R\) form is the most direct when you know V and R.
</p>
<p style="color: #555; font-style: italic; margin-bottom: 0; margin-top: 8px;">
<strong>Concept Tested:</strong> Power Equations \(P = VI = I^2R = V^2/R\)
</p>
</div>
</details>

</div>

<p style="color: #1565C0; font-weight: 700; font-size: 1.08rem; margin-top: 0; margin-bottom: 14px;">Question 4</p>

<p style="color: #333; line-height: 1.75;">
Which SI prefix multiplier corresponds to nano (n)?
</p>

<div class="upper-alpha" markdown>

1. \(10^{-6}\)
2. \(10^{-9}\)
3. \(10^{-12}\)
4. \(10^{-3}\)

</div>

<details style="margin-top: 1rem;">
<summary style="color: #5A3EED; font-weight: 700; cursor: pointer;">Show Answer</summary>
<div style="background: #E7F7E7; border: 2px solid #81C784; border-radius: 10px; padding: 18px 22px; margin-top: 10px;">
<p style="color: #2E7D32; font-weight: 700; margin-top: 0;">Correct Answer: B</p>
<p style="color: #333; line-height: 1.75; margin-bottom: 0;">
Nano (n) = \(10^{-9}\). The common prefixes in order: milli (m) = \(10^{-3}\), micro (μ) = \(10^{-6}\), nano (n) = \(10^{-9}\), pico (p) = \(10^{-12}\). You will encounter these constantly — capacitor values in nanofarads, inductors in nanohenrys, and timing signals measured in nanoseconds.
</p>
<p style="color: #555; font-style: italic; margin-bottom: 0; margin-top: 8px;">
<strong>Concept Tested:</strong> SI Units and Prefixes
</p>
</div>
</details>

<p style="color: #1565C0; font-weight: 700; font-size: 1.08rem; margin-top: 0; margin-bottom: 14px;">Question 5</p>

<p style="color: #333; line-height: 1.75;">
In a circuit, conventional current flows from positive to negative through the external circuit. What is the actual physical direction of electron flow?
</p>

<div class="upper-alpha" markdown>

1. From positive to negative, same as conventional current
2. From negative to positive, opposite to conventional current
3. Electrons do not flow in wires — only protons move
4. The direction depends on whether the source is AC or DC

</div>

<details style="margin-top: 1rem;">
<summary style="color: #5A3EED; font-weight: 700; cursor: pointer;">Show Answer</summary>
<div style="background: #E7F7E7; border: 2px solid #81C784; border-radius: 10px; padding: 18px 22px; margin-top: 10px;">
<p style="color: #2E7D32; font-weight: 700; margin-top: 0;">Correct Answer: B</p>
<p style="color: #333; line-height: 1.75; margin-bottom: 0;">
Electrons flow from negative to positive (opposite to conventional current). Conventional current direction was established before electrons were discovered — scientists guessed positive charges moved, and they guessed wrong. The convention stuck. In circuit analysis we always use conventional current, and the math works out correctly either way.
</p>
<p style="color: #555; font-style: italic; margin-bottom: 0; margin-top: 8px;">
<strong>Concept Tested:</strong> Conventional Current vs. Electron Flow
</p>
</div>
</details>

<p style="color: #1565C0; font-weight: 700; font-size: 1.08rem; margin-top: 0; margin-bottom: 14px;">Question 6</p>

<p style="color: #333; line-height: 1.75;">
What happens to the current in a circuit if the voltage is doubled while the resistance is held constant?
</p>

<div class="upper-alpha" markdown>

1. The current is halved
2. The current is unchanged
3. The current is doubled
4. The current is quadrupled

</div>

<details style="margin-top: 1rem;">
<summary style="color: #5A3EED; font-weight: 700; cursor: pointer;">Show Answer</summary>
<div style="background: #E7F7E7; border: 2px solid #81C784; border-radius: 10px; padding: 18px 22px; margin-top: 10px;">
<p style="color: #2E7D32; font-weight: 700; margin-top: 0;">Correct Answer: C</p>
<p style="color: #333; line-height: 1.75; margin-bottom: 0;">
By Ohm's Law \(I = V/R\), current is directly proportional to voltage when resistance is constant. If \(V\) doubles, then \(I = 2V/R\) — the current doubles as well. This linear relationship is a cornerstone property that makes resistive circuit analysis tractable.
</p>
<p style="color: #555; font-style: italic; margin-bottom: 0; margin-top: 8px;">
<strong>Concept Tested:</strong> Ohm's Law Proportionality
</p>
</div>
</details>

</div>

<p style="color: #1565C0; font-weight: 700; font-size: 1.08rem; margin-top: 0; margin-bottom: 14px;">Question 7</p>

<p style="color: #333; line-height: 1.75;">
What is the key defining difference between an open circuit and a short circuit?
</p>

<div class="upper-alpha" markdown>

1. An open circuit has zero resistance; a short circuit has infinite resistance
2. An open circuit has infinite resistance and zero current; a short circuit has zero resistance and zero voltage across it
3. An open circuit allows maximum current; a short circuit blocks all current
4. An open circuit occurs only in AC systems; a short circuit occurs only in DC systems

</div>

<details style="margin-top: 1rem;">
<summary style="color: #5A3EED; font-weight: 700; cursor: pointer;">Show Answer</summary>
<div style="background: #E7F7E7; border: 2px solid #81C784; border-radius: 10px; padding: 18px 22px; margin-top: 10px;">
<p style="color: #2E7D32; font-weight: 700; margin-top: 0;">Correct Answer: B</p>
<p style="color: #333; line-height: 1.75; margin-bottom: 0;">
An open circuit has infinite resistance: no current flows (\(I = 0\)), but a voltage can exist across the open terminals. A short circuit has zero resistance: the voltage across it is zero (\(V = 0\)), but current is limited only by the rest of the circuit (potentially dangerously high). These are the two extreme cases that bracket all normal circuit operation.
</p>
<p style="color: #555; font-style: italic; margin-bottom: 0; margin-top: 8px;">
<strong>Concept Tested:</strong> Open Circuit and Short Circuit
</p>
</div>
</details>

</div>

<p style="color: #1565C0; font-weight: 700; font-size: 1.08rem; margin-top: 0; margin-bottom: 14px;">Question 8</p>

<p style="color: #333; line-height: 1.75;">
A 9 V battery powers a circuit. Charge of 10 C flows through the battery during operation. How much energy is supplied by the battery?
</p>

<div class="upper-alpha" markdown>

1. 0.9 J
2. 9 J
3. 90 J
4. 1.11 J

</div>

<details style="margin-top: 1rem;">
<summary style="color: #5A3EED; font-weight: 700; cursor: pointer;">Show Answer</summary>
<div style="background: #E7F7E7; border: 2px solid #81C784; border-radius: 10px; padding: 18px 22px; margin-top: 10px;">
<p style="color: #2E7D32; font-weight: 700; margin-top: 0;">Correct Answer: C</p>
<p style="color: #333; line-height: 1.75; margin-bottom: 0;">
From the definition of voltage \(V = W/Q\), we can rearrange to \(W = QV = 10 \text{ C} \times 9 \text{ V} = 90 \text{ J}\). Voltage literally measures how much energy is delivered per coulomb of charge — this calculation makes that concrete. Every coulomb of charge moved through 9 V carries 9 J of energy.
</p>
<p style="color: #555; font-style: italic; margin-bottom: 0; margin-top: 8px;">
<strong>Concept Tested:</strong> Electrical Energy \(W = QV\)
</p>
</div>
</details>
